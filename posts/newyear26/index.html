From EVPN to Emulation: What I’ve Been Actually doing at WGU

It’s been a minute.

I ’ve been wrapping up my degree at WGU recently, and I’ve been meaning to sit down and actually write about it; not just the checkbox of “finished,” but what I actually did along the way. This wasn’t a passive degree. If anything, it ended up reinforcing that I’m still very much a network engineer at heart, even while technically enrolled in a “general IT” program (BSIT).

Still a Network Engineer (Just With Homework Now)

Most of my time at WGU ended up orbiting around networking concepts anyway, intentionally or not.

At work, I’ve been involved in network engineering, and that bled directly into my coursework. Instead of treating assignments like academic exercises, I used them as an excuse to go deeper on things I actually care about: control planes, optics, layer 2, and how real systems behave under pressure.

Two projects stand out.

VXLAN-EVPN Presentation (Excellence Award)

One of my favorite assignments was delivering an oral presentation to stakeholders on VXLAN-EVPN.

Not just “what VXLAN is,” but:

  • why EVPN matters as a control plane
  • how it replaces flood-and-learn behavior
  • how BGP becomes the distribution mechanism for MAC/IP reachability
  • what this actually means for scale, multi-tenancy, and operational sanity

I approached it like I would at work:

  • clear problem framing (why L2 domains don’t scale)
  • architecture diagrams instead of buzzwords
  • tradeoffs (complexity vs. control)
  • and most importantly – what breaks

That assignment ended up earning an Excellence Award, which was quite cool. But more importantly, it validated that going deep instead of wide is still the right move.

Fiber Radius Mandrel Lab (Excellence Award)

The second Excellence Award came from something much more physical: a fiber optic bend radius / mandrel lab.

This one hit differently because it wasn’t abstract:

  • measuring attenuation under different bend conditions
  • seeing real signal degradation instead of just reading about it
  • understanding why bend radius specs actually matter in production environments

It’s one thing to say “don’t exceed minimum bend radius.”
It’s another to watch your signal fall apart when you do.

Again, Excellence Award here, but more importantly, it reinforced something I already knew:
the physical layer still matters, and ignoring it will absolutely burn you.

Certifications Along the Way

WGU also pushed me through a few certifications:

  • LPI Linux Essentials
  • AWS Cloud Practitioner
  • ITIL v4 Foundations

Honestly, each of these filled in different gaps:

  • Linux Essentials reinforced fundamentals I use constantly but rarely formalize
  • AWS Practitioner helped connect infrastructure concepts to cloud abstractions
  • ITIL… gave me a better understanding of why change management exists (even if I still argue with it)

TKO: Rebuilding a Dead Game’s Network Stack

Outside of the structured coursework, the most meaningful project I worked on was for my Web Development Applications (C777) course:

👉 http://tko.hoody.cx

This is a Python-based emulator for Titanic KungFu Offensive, a discontinued Cartoon Network Flash game that originally ran on SmartFoxServer 1.x.

What started as a “python web dev project” quickly turned into a full-blown network reverse engineering effort.

From the README: https://github.com/pwnedbytoxic/TKO

Key pieces I rebuilt:

  • Flash policy server
  • SmartFox TCP socket emulation
  • BlueBox HTTP tunneling
  • Lobby, matchmaking, and session flow
  • Server-authoritative game simulation
  • Packet reconstruction from decompiled ActionScript

This wasn’t just “make it run”—it was:

  • reverse engineering packet formats
  • reconstructing timing behavior
  • making the server authoritative instead of trusting the client
  • fixing edge cases that only show up mid-match

Basically: real networking problems, just inside a 2000s Flash game.

The repo isn’t public (yet), but here’s the upstream:

  • https://github.com/pwnedbytoxic/TKO

And there’s an active community we’ve cultivated working on it here:

  • https://discord.gg/Qu6yAkRag4

700k Views Later

At some point, I posted a clip of the netcode work on Twitter.

It ended up pulling 700k views, which I definitely didn’t expect.

https://x.com/BenTenNews/status/2035921797988642831?s=20

I think it resonated because:

  • it’s not often you see SmartFoxServer-era infrastructure brought back to life
  • and even less often rebuilt in a modern, server-authoritative way

It was a nice reminder that: deep technical work still gets attention when it’s visible.

What WGU Actually Did For Me

WGU didn’t turn me into a network engineer, I already was one, even if not yet in title.

What it did do:

  • forced structure around things I might’ve otherwise skipped
  • pushed me to document and present ideas clearly
  • gave me an excuse to formalize knowledge I’d built informally
  • and occasionally made me step outside my comfort zone (looking at you, ITIL)

But the biggest takeaway so far?

I never stopped doing real engineering while earning the degree.

If anything, I doubled down on it:

  • VXLAN/EVPN deep dives
  • physical layer validation
  • protocol emulation
  • multiplayer netcode reconstruction

That’s the part I’m most proud of.

What’s Next

More of the same, honestly.

  • Keep pushing deeper into network architecture
  • Focus on CCNP ENCOR as soon as I graduate
  • Keep building weird things (like resurrecting dead games)
  • Keep bridging the gap between “academic IT” and “real systems”

And probably:

  • make the TKO repo public once it’s a bit cleaner & fleshed out

If you’ve read this far, you probably already get it.

This wasn’t about finishing an IT degree.

It was about building things the whole time anyway.
