College Graduation & Getting Into Cisco Voice

It has been a busy couple of months since finishing my capstone.

I officially graduated from Western Governors University with my B.S. in Information Technology, and I also started a new position as a Network Support Specialist at NEOnet. The capstone ended up being a fitting final project for the degree; I built a multi-site campus network, discussed it during my interview, and then moved into a position where I am now supporting network infrastructure professionally.

The biggest change has been the type of networking I am learning. Most of my experience before this point was centered around route/switch, campus networks, security, and the usual infrastructure services sitting behind them. I am still working with all of that, but I have also started digging much further into Cisco Unified Communications and how voice actually moves through a network.

I knew VoIP still relied on switching, routing, DHCP, DNS, and QoS, but that was about where my practical understanding stopped. Once I started looking at CUCM, SIP call flows, dial plans, gateways, and media negotiation, it became obvious that voice has its own routing logic layered on top of the IP network underneath it.

Finishing WGU

Graduating from WGU feels less dramatic than I expected, mostly because the transition into my new position happened so quickly. I submitted the capstone, passed, completed the remaining graduation steps, and then moved directly into learning an entirely new area of networking.

Still, finishing the degree means a lot to me.

When I chose the BSIT instead of the Network Engineering and Security program, I wanted the freedom to shape a general IT degree around the work I was already doing. That ended up being the right decision. I used projects to explore VXLAN-EVPN, fiber attenuation, Linux, network services, protocol emulation, and eventually the Cisco CML campus network that became my capstone.

The program gave me enough room to make the work relevant instead of treating every assignment like an isolated academic exercise. It also forced me to document and explain what I built, which has probably been more useful professionally than memorizing another list of terms for an objective assessment.

My capstone was the clearest example of that. It was not a production network or a full SD-WAN deployment, but it was large enough to expose real design and troubleshooting problems. I had to work through BGP, centralized DHCP and DNS, ACL behavior, syslog, WAN impairment, and the difference between what the topology actually proved and what I originally wanted it to prove.

That project also came up during my interview at NEOnet, so it ended up serving as more than a graduation requirement. It gave me a complete technical story to talk through: what I designed, why I made certain decisions, what broke, and where I deliberately limited the scope.

Starting at NEOnet

My new position as a Network Support Specialist has been a major step forward because networking is now the center of my work rather than one responsibility mixed in with everything else.

I am still very early in the position, and I have a lot to learn about the systems and environments I will be supporting. I also need to be careful about what I discuss publicly, so this blog will not turn into a running diary of production configurations or customer issues. What I can write about are the technologies themselves, the labs I build around them, and the concepts I am learning as I become more familiar with voice.

So far, Cisco voice has been the largest new subject.

It is easy to describe a phone as another endpoint connected to a switch, but the network is only one part of what makes it useful. A Cisco phone has to learn its voice VLAN, receive its network configuration, locate the appropriate services, download its configuration, and register with call control before it can do much of anything. After that, CUCM still has to decide what the user is allowed to dial, how the digits should be interpreted, and where the call needs to go.

There is a surprising amount happening behind a user picking up a handset and dialing four numbers.

How a Cisco Phone Gets Online

The initial phone-registration process has been one of the better entry points into understanding the rest of the system because it connects familiar networking services with Cisco-specific voice behavior.

After a phone powers on, it can learn the voice VLAN through CDP or LLDP-MED from the access switch. It then requests an address through DHCP, commonly receiving information such as its IP address, default gateway, DNS servers, and the location of its TFTP service through DHCP option 150.

From there, the phone contacts TFTP and downloads the configuration generated for it by CUCM. That configuration can include the phone’s assigned device pool, CUCM servers, firmware information, directory numbers, button layout, security settings, and other device-specific parameters. Once the phone has what it needs, it attempts to register with one of its configured call-control nodes.

This is where troubleshooting starts to become more interesting than checking whether the switchport is up. A phone can have power but never learn the correct voice VLAN. It can receive an address but get the wrong TFTP server. It can reach TFTP but receive an invalid configuration, have a certificate issue, or fail to establish signaling with CUCM.

The symptoms tend to look similar from the desk – the 7841 never finishes registering – but the actual problem can exist at several different stages of the process.

CUCM and the Dial Plan

Once a phone is registered, the CUCM dial plan determines what happens when someone starts dialing.

This has probably been my favorite part of voice so far because it feels similar to routing, although the prefixes are telephone numbers and the forwarding policy is spread across CUCM objects. Instead of simply finding the longest IP prefix in a routing table, CUCM performs digit analysis and evaluates whether the calling device is allowed to reach the matching destination.

A directory number, route pattern, translation pattern, voicemail pilot, or other destination can be placed inside a partition. The caller’s Calling Search Space, or CSS, defines which partitions it is allowed to search and in what order. That relationship controls reachability inside the dial plan.

For example, two phones may both be registered correctly and have perfectly functional network connectivity, but one may be unable to call a particular destination because its CSS does not include the partition containing that number. Nothing is wrong with IP routing; the call is being denied by the logical search space inside CUCM.

External calls introduce more decisions. A route pattern can match the dialed digits and direct the call toward a route list, which contains one or more route groups. Those groups can then select a SIP trunk, CUBE, or another gateway capable of carrying the call outside the cluster.

Digit manipulation can happen at several points along that path. Translation patterns may normalize what the user dials, while calling- and called-party transformations can change how numbers are presented to another system. A user might dial a short access code and ten-digit number, while the provider eventually receives a normalized +E.164 number.

This is also where a badly designed dial plan can become difficult to reason through. Partitions, CSSs, route patterns, route lists, transformations, and trunks all make sense independently; the challenge is following the call through the entire sequence and understanding which object changed the digits or selected the next hop.

SIP Signaling and the Media Path

I have also been spending a lot of time learning SIP, particularly the difference between call signaling and the media that carries the actual conversation.

A basic SIP call begins with an INVITE, followed by responses that indicate the request is being processed, the destination is ringing, and the call has been answered. The exact flow varies depending on the systems involved, but a simplified exchange might include:

INVITE
100 Trying
180 Ringing
200 OK
ACK

The SIP messages establish, modify, and eventually tear down the session. The accompanying SDP content describes the proposed media, including the address and ports where an endpoint expects to receive RTP, the codecs it supports, and the methods available for handling things such as DTMF.

Once both sides agree, the video/audio is normally carried through RTP.

The coolest thing? This isn’t just for phones. I’m supporting SIP ringers, IP video, IoT, IM & Presence, and much more for hundreds of clients.

One important thing I had to correct in my own mental model was the assumption that all audio passes through CUCM. CUCM controls and routes the call, but it usually does not sit directly inside the RTP media path. Depending on the call, the endpoints may send RTP directly to one another, through CUBE, through a media resource, Cisco Emergency Reponder, or through some other anchored point in the design.

That separation explains why a call can appear successful from a signaling perspective while having broken audio. The phones may ring, the user may answer, and the call timer may start because SIP completed normally. Meanwhile, an incorrect SDP address, blocked UDP range, NAT problem, or missing return route can prevent RTP from reaching one of the endpoints.

One-way audio is a good example. It is tempting to treat it as a phone problem, but it often means the signaling path completed while one direction of the media path did not. Troubleshooting it requires looking at the SDP exchanged during setup, identifying the addresses and ports each side advertised, and then checking whether RTP can actually follow those paths in both directions.

CUBE and IOS XE Voice Routing

Cisco Unified Border Element, or CUBE, has been another major area of study.

CUBE acts as a border between voice systems, often sitting between CUCM and a SIP provider or between separate collaboration environments. Rather than transparently passing the original SIP session through, it operates as a back-to-back user agent and creates separate call legs on either side. It’s also just a router.

That gives it room to handle differences between the connected systems. CUBE can modify numbers, manipulate SIP headers, negotiate codecs, anchor media, separate internal addressing from external systems, and provide a controlled demarcation point for SIP traffic.

The IOS XE configuration underneath it introduces dial peers, which determine how incoming and outgoing calls are matched and handled. A dial peer may match based on called digits, calling digits, SIP URI information, an incoming interface, or other characteristics of the call. After matching an inbound dial peer, the gateway has to select the appropriate outbound dial peer and build the next call leg.

This can get complicated quickly because the configuration is not only deciding where to send an IP packet. It is evaluating an entire call, potentially translating the calling or called number, selecting a codec, applying a SIP profile, negotiating DTMF, and determining which upstream server or trunk should receive it.

The troubleshooting questions become very specific:

  • Which inbound dial peer matched the call?
  • Which outbound dial peer was selected?
  • Were the calling and called numbers translated as expected?
  • Did both sides offer a compatible codec?
  • Did the SDP advertise a reachable media address?
  • Was a SIP header missing or formatted differently than the far side expected?
  • Did the provider reject the call, or did the gateway fail before sending it?

Those are the parts of voice that feel most familiar to me. It is still protocol analysis, packet captures, routing decisions, and configuration logic; the difference is that the result is a phone call rather than a web session or ping response.

Media Resources, Codecs, and DTMF

Not every endpoint or trunk agrees on how media should be handled, so I have also been learning where CUCM media resources fit into a call.

A transcoder can convert between codecs when two call legs do not share a usable format. A conference bridge mixes audio from multiple participants, while a Media Termination Point can provide media termination or help resolve certain signaling and capability mismatches.

These resources are not automatically required for every call, and using them changes the path the media takes. That makes it important to understand why CUCM inserted a resource instead of only confirming that the call connected.

Codec selection is one example. A call using G.711 consumes more bandwidth than one using a compressed codec, but avoiding G.711 is not always automatically better. The selected codec affects bandwidth, audio quality, transcoder use, and sometimes feature compatibility. The correct choice depends on where the call is going and what the available path can support.

DTMF has its own negotiation considerations. Two people can hear each other perfectly while an automated menu ignores every keypress because the systems disagree about how DTMF should be transported. Depending on the call, tones may be sent in the audio stream, through RTP events, or through SIP signaling.

That is a small detail until someone needs to enter a conference PIN or navigate voicemail.

Using CLCOR as a Study Outline

I have been using the 350-801 CLCOR blueprint as a map for what to study, even though my immediate goal is understanding the environment rather than rushing toward an exam date. I will hopefully get my CCNP one of these years :)

The blueprint covers a broad amount of the Cisco collaboration stack: CUCM architecture, endpoint registration, SIP and SDP, dial-plan configuration, CUBE, IOS XE gateways, media resources, QoS, Unity Connection, security, automation, and Cisco’s cloud and hybrid collaboration services.

Some of those areas are already becoming more familiar through work and study, while others are still mostly names on a diagram. I have spent more time on call routing, SIP, dial peers, CUBE, codecs, and media behavior than I have on topics such as Unity Connection, Expressway, or Webex integrations.

That unevenness is fine for now. The blueprint gives me a structured way to identify the gaps without pretending I need to understand the entire collaboration ecosystem at once.

It also reflects something I have noticed while learning voice: call control cannot really be studied as one isolated application. A production collaboration environment can involve CUCM clusters, gateways, CUBE, voicemail, certificates, directory integration, DNS, NTP, QoS, cloud services, and several different media paths. Understanding the individual platforms helps, but the more difficult skill is following a user’s call across all of them.

A Change in Direction

A lot of the older content on this site focuses on cybersecurity. I started with CTFs, practical ethical hacking, network-security labs, and the idea that I would eventually move into a security-focused engineering role.

I do not consider that time wasted, and I am not planning to remove those posts. Security taught me to inspect traffic, understand protocol behavior, question trust boundaries, and pay attention to what a system is actually doing instead of what the diagram says it should be doing.

All of that still applies to voice. SIP trunks need to be secured, certificates and TLS matter, firewall policy can break signaling or media, and an overly permissive dial plan can become an avenue for toll fraud. Voice is not replacing the networking and security knowledge I already have; it is giving that knowledge a more specific direction.

At this point, I am less interested in pursuing cybersecurity as a separate destination and more interested in becoming stronger at the infrastructure that carries communication. That includes route/switch, network operations, call control, SIP, gateways, and whatever else I need to understand to troubleshoot the network from end to end.

My earlier capstone post described the move toward VoIP as a pivot rather than a reset, and that still holds up. I am using the same troubleshooting habits, but applying them to a much deeper voice stack than I understood before.

What’s Next

The blog will probably start reflecting this change as I get further into the material.

I want to build out a voice lab where I can document CUCM registration, partitions and Calling Search Spaces, route patterns, digit manipulation, SIP trunks, CUBE, dial-peer matching, and media troubleshooting without involving any production systems. I would also like to spend more time reading SIP ladders and packet captures, since that seems like the most direct way to understand what a failed call was actually trying to do.

There will still be route/switch and infrastructure content. Voice depends on those systems, and I am nowhere near finished learning them. The difference is that I now have a new application sitting on top of the network, with its own control plane, routing logic, and very audible failure modes.

For now, I am grateful to have finished WGU, grateful for the opportunity at NEOnet, and excited to keep learning something that was entirely outside my experience a few months ago.

Thanks for reading, and God bless.
